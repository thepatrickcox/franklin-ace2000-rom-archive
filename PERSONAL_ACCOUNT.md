# Personal Account: Working with Franklin Computers EPROMs and Prototypes, 1982–1984

**Patrick Cox**  
Columbus / Bordentown / Pennsauken, New Jersey area

Between 1982 and 1984 I worked for Franklin Computers in Pennsauken, New Jersey. I worked in the basement of our house in Columbus, New Jersey on computers. I learned how to program like any other kid from the back of magazines. The one I did the most work on was an Apple IIe in RUN and BRUN mode (of course), learning how to make the computer do various things. I loved playing chess, and that 5¼" floppy and the Franklin computer worked me out pretty hard. I finally was able to play and beat college professors as a young teen.

I had two 5¼" floppy double drives, and that made me hot stuff. I had a daisy-chain printer with that ball, and I even had a modem — you stuck the analog phone handset on the acoustic coupler. I rarely used it.

I went to church in Bordentown, New Jersey at the Presbyterian Church on Main Street. Reverend Ernie Olsen was the minister at the time. My mother would drag us there every Sunday morning, and we were there early. John Applegate was our Sunday school teacher at that time. He was also a middle school teacher, and he started doing extracurricular activities with us like canoeing and hiking and things like that.

My mother told John that I was a computer person and was doing some programming in the basement. He was the brother of Bob Applegate, who worked at Franklin Computers in Pennsauken, New Jersey as a computer scientist and programmer. When John found this out, they hired me on, and I tested and worked with the ROMs from 1982 to 1984.

I would test the 5¼" floppies with their prototypes and use them to test and assess read/write software functionality using various software packages. I tested the programs, documenting whether things went well, what went wrong, the syntax errors that came up, and so on. I did this for several years.

They gave me computers to take home — computers that I had in my room. Some of them had markings and some didn’t. I remember changing the DIP switches to make the different EPROMs work.

John Applegate was a middle school teacher, and then he started to work with Bob Applegate. I remember Bob teaching me and showing me how they imaged the EPROMs. I remember his cartoon drawings on his desk. I remember him being a really good teacher and a brilliant man. I learned so much. A lot of what I learned from him allowed me to excel in other areas of my life.

In 1994 I became a vision system programmer in Clinton, South Carolina, working for Avery Dennison. I put Acuity vision systems with VME buses and controls on Comco printing machines to control the Duracell Powercheck product. We were making it up as we went. I was on the Duracell Powercheck Avery research and development design team out of Pasadena, California. We designed graphical user interfaces (remember Wonderware?) with touchscreens for the operators and controlled the operations — PLCs, TCP/IP protocols, the works.

After that, I worked as an Automation Engineer in the semiconductor industry, working in my own company doing vision system and control designs for companies like Kemet with their communications industry anodes and Champion Aviation with their spark plugs. Then I worked in the semiconductor field with dry film resist for Morton Electronic Materials, then Shipley Ronal company, and finally Rohm and Haas. I traveled the world automating computer systems and robots for the semiconductor industry.

All of this started because of the boy in the basement who met Bob Applegate at Franklin Computers.

**Note on these specific artifacts (added 2026)**  
The chips photographed and dumped in June 2026 (the ones bearing the factory ©1984,1985 Franklin REV B labels and the hand-marked P1 12-12, P2 12-12, and P3 8-6 designations) are examples of the type of development EPROMs I worked with and tested during my time at Franklin. These particular units had been stored as relics in a case with other artifacts until this week, when I extracted them on a T48 reader for the first time. The binary images and analysis in this repository come directly from those extractions.

This personal account is included at the request of historians I have spoken with, who felt the first-hand context of how these parts were actually used and tested during the critical 1982–1984 period would be valuable to the historical record.
